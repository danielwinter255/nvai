The Impact of Climate Change on Ocean Ecosystems

By: Sarah Johnson | Period 3 | Environmental Science

Introduction

Climate change is one of the most pressing issues facing our planet today. The effects of rising temperatures and increased carbon dioxide levels are particularly evident in our oceans, where marine ecosystems are experiencing unprecedented changes. This essay will explore the various ways climate change affects ocean life and what we can do to mitigate these impacts.

Ocean Acidification

Ocean acidification, caused by the absorption of CO2, is making it harder for shellfish and coral to build their calcium carbonate shells and skeletons. Since the industrial revolution, ocean acidity has increased by approximately 30%. This has cascading effects throughout the food chain, affecting everything from tiny plankton to large marine mammals.

Coral Bleaching

Rising sea temperatures are also causing coral bleaching events, where stressed corals expel the algae living in their tissues. Without these symbiotic algae, corals turn white and often die, destroying habitats that support thousands of species. The Great Barrier Reef has experienced multiple mass bleaching events in recent years.

Migration Patterns

The migration patterns of many fish species are changing as they seek cooler waters, disrupting traditional fishing communities and marine food webs. Species that once thrived in certain regions are moving toward the poles, while invasive species take their place in warming waters.

Conclusion

In conclusion, climate change poses a significant threat to ocean ecosystems worldwide. From acidification to coral bleaching to shifting migration patterns, the impacts are far-reaching and interconnected. Urgent action is needed to reduce carbon emissions and protect our oceans for future generations.
